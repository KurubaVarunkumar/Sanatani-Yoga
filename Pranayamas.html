Pranayama is the practice of breath control in yoga. The term is derived from the Sanskrit words "prana," meaning life force or vital energy, and "ayama," meaning control or expansion. In yogic philosophy, prana is believed to be the subtle energy that animates and sustains life. Pranayama techniques involve conscious regulation of the breath to enhance the flow of prana in the body.

Some of Pranayamas given below

1.Ujjayani Pranayama :

Also known as the "Victorious Breath" or "Ocean Breath," Ujjayi involves breathing slowly and deeply through the nose, creating a sound in the back of the throat that resembles the ocean.

how to do

**Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position or lie down in a relaxed posture. Inhale deeply and slowly through your nose. Constrict the back of your throat as you exhale slowly through your nose. As you exhale, produce a soft "haa" sound in the back of your throat. The sound is similar to the sound of the ocean or a light snoring noise. Inhale and exhale evenly, focusing on making the breath smooth and controlled. Feel the sensation of the breath at the back of your throat.

2.Kapalbhati Pranayama:

Kapalbhati Pranayama is a yogic breathing exercise that is part of the ancient Indian practice of Hatha Yoga. The name "Kapalbhati" is derived from two Sanskrit words: "kapal," meaning skull or forehead, and "bhati," meaning shining or illuminating. Therefore, Kapalbhati is often translated as "Skull Shining Breath" or "Shining Skull Breath."

how to do

  • Sitting Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with an erect spine. Ensure that your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Hand Placement: Place your hands on your knees with palms facing upward. You can also use the Jnana Mudra, where you touch the tip of your index finger to the tip of your thumb, while keeping the other three fingers extended.
  • Focus on Breath:Take a few deep breaths to relax and center yourself. Kapalbhati involves short, powerful exhales, emphasizing the active exhalation while keeping the inhalation passive.
  • Kapalbhati Technique:Inhale comfortably and then forcefully exhale through the nose by contracting the abdominal muscles. The emphasis is on the exhalation, and the inhalation should happen naturally as a result of releasing the abdominal contraction. The exhale should be active, quick, and rhythmic, with a short, sharp burst of breath. The inhalation is more passive and occurs naturally as the abdomen relaxes.
  • Repetitions: Start with a few rounds of Kapalbhati, gradually increasing the pace and duration as your comfort level and proficiency improve. A typical session may consist of 50 to 100 rapid breaths, followed by a deep inhalation and exhalation.
  • Ending the Practice: After completing the rounds, take a few deep breaths and allow your breath to return to its natural rhythm. Observe any sensations in your body.
    • 2.Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril Breathing):

    Nadi Shodhana, also known as Alternate Nostril Breathing or Anulom Vilom, is a popular pranayama (breathing) technique in yoga. The term "Nadi Shodhana" translates to "channel purification" in Sanskrit, referring to the energetic channels through which prana (life force) flows. This practice is designed to balance the flow of prana in the body and calm the mind.

    how to do

  • Sitting Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with an erect spine. Ensure that your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Hand Placement: Use the right thumb to close off the right nostril and the ring finger or the pinky finger to close off the left nostril. The index and middle fingers can rest gently on the forehead or on the space between the eyebrows.
  • Breathing Pattern: Begin by taking a few deep breaths to relax. Close the right nostril with the thumb and inhale slowly and deeply through the left nostril. After the inhalation, close the left nostril with the ring or pinky finger, release the right nostril, and exhale slowly and completely through the right nostril. Inhale again through the right nostril, then close it, release the left nostril, and exhale through the left nostril. This completes one cycle. Continue alternating nostrils for several rounds.
  • Ratio: In the beginning, focus on maintaining a comfortable and balanced rhythm. As you become more proficient, you can gradually increase the duration of inhalation, exhalation, and retention.
  • Ending the Practice: After completing several rounds, finish with an exhalation through the left nostril. Release the hand mudra and allow the breath to return to its natural rhythm.
    • 4.Bhramari Pranayama (Humming Bee):

    Bhramari Pranayama, often referred to as the "Humming Bee Breath," is a calming and meditative breathing practice in yoga. The word "Bhramari" is derived from the Sanskrit word "bhramar," which means a bee. This pranayama involves creating a humming sound during exhalation, resembling the gentle buzzing of a bee. Bhramari is known for its ability to quiet the mind and induce a sense of inner peace.

    how to do

  • Sitting Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with an erect spine. Ensure that your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Hand Placement: You can choose between two hand mudras for Bhramari: Option 1: Place your index fingers on the cartilage between your cheeks and ears, thumbs covering your eyes, and other fingers resting on your forehead. Option 2: Alternatively, you can use the Shanmukhi Mudra, where you close your ears with your thumbs, eyes with your index fingers, nostrils with your middle fingers, and lips with your ring fingers. The pinky fingers rest on the lower part of your face.
  • Breath Awareness: Close your eyes and take a few deep breaths to relax.
  • Humming Sound:Inhale deeply through your nose. Exhale slowly and smoothly while making a deep humming sound. Focus on feeling the vibration of the sound. Allow the humming sound to be continuous and steady throughout the exhalation. The exhale should be active, quick, and rhythmic, with a short, sharp burst of breath. The inhalation is more passive and occurs naturally as the abdomen relaxes.
  • Duration: Start with a few rounds and gradually increase the duration as you become more comfortable. You can practice Bhramari for 5-10 minutes, depending on your preference.
  • Mindful Awareness: While practicing, cultivate a sense of mindful awareness, paying attention to the vibrations and the calming effect of the humming sound.
  • Ending the Practice: After the final round, release your hands and sit quietly for a few moments, observing any sensations or changes in your mental state.
    • 5.Anulom Vilom (Nadi Shodhana Variation):

    Anulom Vilom is a pranayama technique also known as "Alternate Nostril Breathing." It is a popular and versatile breathing exercise in yoga that helps balance the flow of energy in the body, enhance respiratory function, and bring about a sense of calm and balance. Anulom Vilom is similar to Nadi Shodhana (Alternate Nostril Breathing) but has some variations in the technique.

    how to do

  • Sitting Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with an erect spine. Ensure that your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Hand Position (Mudra): Use the right thumb to close off the right nostril and the right ring finger or pinky finger to close off the left nostril. The other fingers can rest gently on your forehead..
  • Anulom Vilom Technique: Start by closing your right nostril with the right thumb and inhaling slowly and deeply through the left nostril. After the inhalation, close the left nostril with the right ring finger or pinky finger, release the right nostril, and exhale slowly and completely through the right nostril. Inhale again through the right nostril, then close it, release the left nostril, and exhale through the left nostril. This completes one cycle. Continue alternating nostrils for several rounds.
  • Smooth and Controlled Breathing: Focus on making the breath smooth, controlled, and without any force. The inhalation and exhalation should be of equal duration.
  • As you become comfortable with the practice, you can gradually increase the duration of inhalation, exhalation, and the retention between breaths.
  • Ending the Practice: After completing several rounds, finish with an exhalation through the left nostril. Release the hand mudra and allow the breath to return to its natural rhythm.
    • 6.Sheetali Pranayama:

    Sheetali Pranayama, also known as Cooling Breath, is a yoga breathing technique that is designed to cool the body and calm the mind. "Sheetali" comes from the Sanskrit word "Sheetal," which means cool or soothing. This pranayama is particularly useful for reducing stress, anxiety, and excess heat in the body.

    how to do

  • Preparation: Find a comfortable seated position. You can sit on the floor with your legs crossed (in Sukhasana) or on a chair with your feet flat on the ground. Ensure that your spine is straight, and your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Mudra (Hand Gesture): Adopt a mudra called Vishnu Mudra. Curl the sides of your tongue to form a tube-like shape. If you are unable to curl your tongue, you can simply press the tip of your tongue against the back of your front teeth. Place your hands on your knees with the palms facing upward. Close your eyes to enhance your focus inward.
  • Inhalation: Inhale slowly and deeply through the rolled tongue (or the slightly open mouth if you cannot roll your tongue). Feel the coolness of the breath as it passes through the tongue. Inhale to a comfortable capacity without straining.
  • Retention: After a full inhalation, close your mouth and hold the breath for a few moments. This is the retention phase.
  • Exhalation: Exhale slowly and completely through the nostrils. Focus on the warm sensation of the breath as it leaves your body. This completes one round.
  • Repeat: Continue for several rounds, gradually increasing the duration of inhalation, retention, and exhalation as you become more comfortable with the practice. You can start with 5 rounds and gradually increase to 10 or more as you progress.
    • 7.Sitkari Pranayama:

    Sitkari Pranayama is another yogic breathing technique that involves inhaling through the teeth. This practice is believed to have a cooling and calming effect on the body and mind, similar to Sheetali Pranayama. The term "Sitkari" is derived from the Sanskrit words "Sit" meaning "cool" and "kari" meaning "to make" or "to do." This pranayama is particularly useful for reducing excess heat in the body and promoting a sense of tranquility.

    how to do

  • Preparation: Find a comfortable seated position. You can sit on the floor with your legs crossed (in Sukhasana) or on a chair with your feet flat on the ground. Ensure that your spine is straight, and your shoulders are relaxed.
  • Mudra (Hand Gesture): Close your eyes and bring your lower and upper teeth together without clenching them. Leave a small space between your lips.
  • Inhalation: Inhale slowly and deeply through the small gap between your upper and lower teeth. Focus on the sound of the breath as it passes through the teeth. Inhale to a comfortable capacity without straining.
  • Retention: After a full inhalation, close your mouth and hold the breath for a few moments. This is the retention phase.
  • Exhalation: Exhale slowly and completely through the nostrils. Focus on the warm sensation of the breath as it leaves your body. This completes one round.
  • Repeat: Continue for several rounds, gradually increasing the duration of inhalation, retention, and exhalation as you become more comfortable with the practice. You can start with 5 rounds and gradually increase to 10 or more as you progress.
    • 8.Bhastrika Pranayama:

    Bhastrika Pranayama, often referred to as Bellows Breath, is a powerful and energizing yogic breathing technique. The name "Bhastrika" is derived from the Sanskrit word "Bhastrika," which means bellows. This pranayama involves rapid and forceful inhalations and exhalations, creating a bellows-like movement in the abdomen. Bhastrika Pranayama is known for its ability to increase energy, improve mental clarity, and enhance lung capacity.

    how to do

  • Preparation: Sit in a comfortable and stable posture, such as Sukhasana (cross-legged) or Padmasana (lotus pose). Keep your spine straight and shoulders relaxed. Place your hands on your knees in a mudra (gesture) of your choice, such as Jnana Mudra (touching the tip of the thumb to the index finger with the other fingers extended).
  • Breathing Technique: Take a few deep breaths to prepare. Inhale deeply through both nostrils, expanding your chest and filling your lungs with air. Exhale forcefully and completely through both nostrils, contracting your abdomen. This should create a pumping or bellows-like movement.
  • Rhythm: Establish a rapid and rhythmic pattern of breath, with equal emphasis on both inhalation and exhalation. Beginners can start with a moderate pace and gradually increase the speed as they become more comfortable.
  • Duration: Practice Bhastrika for about 1-2 minutes initially, gradually increasing the duration as you build stamina. Take a few moments of rest with normal breathing after each round.
  • Awareness: Keep your awareness on the breath and the rhythmic movement of the abdomen. Maintain a focused and alert state of mind.
  • Number of Rounds: Start with 3 rounds and gradually increase to 5 or more as you become accustomed to the practice..
    • Benefits of Bhastrika Pranayama:
  • Increased Energy: Bhastrika is known for its invigorating and energizing effects on the body.
  • Improved Lung Capacity: Strengthens the respiratory system and enhances lung function.
  • Clears the Mind: Helps in clearing mental fog and improving concentration.
  • Clears the Mind: Helps in clearing mental fog and improving concentration.
    • Contraindications:
  • Individuals with high blood pressure, heart conditions, or respiratory issues should practice Bhastrika Pranayama with caution or consult a healthcare professional before attempting.
  • Pregnant women and individuals with abdominal injuries or surgeries should avoid or modify the practice.
    • 9.Dirgha Pranayama (Three-Part Breath):

    Dirgha Pranayama, also known as Three-Part Breath or Complete Breath, is a foundational yogic breathing technique that encourages full and mindful breathing. The practice involves consciously filling the three parts of the lungs with breath, promoting relaxation, stress reduction, and increased awareness of breath. It is a great technique for beginners and can be done in a comfortable seated position.

    how to do

  • Seated Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with your spine erect. You can also practice this lying down if sitting is uncomfortable.
  • Hands Placement: Place your hands on your abdomen. This helps you feel the movement of your breath in the first part of the practice.
  • Part 1 - Abdominal Breath: Inhale deeply and slowly through your nose, allowing your abdomen to expand fully. Feel your diaphragm moving downward, creating space for the breath. Exhale completely, allowing your abdomen to contract. Feel the gentle rise and fall of your hands on your abdomen.
  • Part 2 - Thoracic Breath: After the abdominal breath, continue inhaling to expand your ribcage. Feel your chest lift and expand horizontally. Exhale slowly, allowing your chest to naturally contract.
  • Part 3 - Clavicular Breath: After the thoracic breath, inhale a bit more, allowing the breath to reach the uppermost part of your lungs. Lift your collarbones and shoulders slightly. Exhale slowly, allowing your shoulders and collarbones to relax.
  • Complete Breath Cycle: Inhale smoothly, filling the abdomen, ribcage, and upper chest sequentially. Exhale slowly and completely, releasing the breath in the reverse order: upper chest, ribcage, and then abdomen.
  • Rhythmic Breathing: Establish a smooth, continuous rhythm without forcing the breath. Aim for a natural and relaxed flow.
  • Awareness: Keep your awareness on the breath and the three distinct parts of the breath cycle.
  • Duration: Practice for 5-10 minutes initially, gradually extending the duration as you become more comfortable.
    • Benefits of Dirgha Pranayama:
  • Stress Reduction: Promotes relaxation and helps alleviate stress and anxiety.
  • Increased Lung Capacity: Enhances lung function and capacity.
  • Mind-Body Connection: Cultivates awareness of the breath and connects the mind and body.
  • Balancing: Balances the autonomic nervous system.
    Contraindications:
  • Dirgha Pranayama is generally safe for most individuals. However, if you have respiratory conditions or other health concerns, it's advisable to consult a healthcare professional before beginning any pranayama practice.
    • 10.Surya Bhedana Pranayama:

    Surya Bhedana Pranayama, also known as Right Nostril Breathing, is a yogic breathing technique that involves the inhalation through the right nostril while keeping the left nostril closed. This pranayama is believed to have a heating and energizing effect on the body. The term "Surya Bhedana" translates to "piercing the sun" in Sanskrit, suggesting an activation of solar or heating energy.

    how to do

  • Seated Position: Sit comfortably in a cross-legged position on the floor or on a chair with your spine erect. You can also practice this lying down if sitting is uncomfortable.
  • Hand Position (Mudra): Use the Vishnu Mudra: Close the index and middle fingers of your right hand into your palm. Use your thumb to close your right nostril and your ring finger to close your left nostril.
  • Exhale: Begin with a few normal breaths to center yourself. Exhale completely.
  • Inhalation (Right Nostril): Close your left nostril with your ring finger. Inhale deeply and slowly through your right nostril. Focus on the sensation of the breath as it enters the body.
  • Retention: After inhaling, release the left nostril and close the right nostril with your thumb. Hold the breath for a brief moment (retention).
  • Exhalation (Left Nostril): Exhale slowly and completely through your left nostril. Focus on the sensation of the breath leaving the body.
  • Repeat: This completes one round. Continue for several rounds, gradually increasing the duration of inhalation, retention, and exhalation.
  • Smooth Transition: Aim for a smooth transition between inhalation, retention, and exhalation, creating a continuous and rhythmic flow.
    • Benefits of Surya Bhedana Pranayama:
  • Energizing Effect: Activates the sympathetic nervous system, providing a feeling of alertness and energy.
  • Heat Generation: Generates heat in the body, which can be beneficial during cold weather.
  • Mental Clarity: Helps improve focus and concentration.
  • Balances the Nadis: According to yogic philosophy, Surya Bhedana is believed to balance the Pingala Nadi, associated with the sun and heating energy..
    Contraindications:
  • Individuals with high blood pressure or heart conditions should practice Surya Bhedana Pranayama with caution or consult a healthcare professional before attempting.
  • If you experience dizziness or discomfort, return to normal breathing and discontinue the practice.

    • 12.Kumbhaka (Breath Retention) Pranayama:

    Kumbhaka, or breath retention, is a component of various pranayama practices in yoga. It involves intentionally holding the breath after inhalation (Antara Kumbhaka) or after exhalation (Bahya Kumbhaka). The practice of Kumbhaka has been an integral part of traditional yogic techniques, and it is often incorporated into more advanced pranayama routines.

    Types of Kumbhaka Pranayama

    Antara Kumbhaka (Internal Breath Retention):
    This involves holding the breath after inhalation. After a deep inhalation, the practitioner closes the glottis (the space between the vocal cords) and holds the breath for a specific duration. This type of breath retention is associated with building internal heat and energy.
    Bahya Kumbhaka (External Breath Retention):
    This involves holding the breath after exhalation. After exhaling completely, the practitioner holds the breath out by closing the glottis. Bahya Kumbhaka is often associated with stilling the mind and promoting a sense of calmness.

    how to do

  • Preparation: Start by finding a comfortable seated position with a straight spine. You can use a chair or sit on the floor in a cross-legged posture. Begin with some rounds of deep and conscious breathing to prepare your respiratory system.
  • Antara Kumbhaka (Internal Breath Retention): Inhale deeply and slowly through your nose. After a full inhalation, close your glottis, holding the breath inside. Maintain a comfortable and steady retention without straining. When you are ready to release, exhale slowly and completely.
  • Bahya Kumbhaka (External Breath Retention): Exhale completely through your nose. After a full exhalation, close your glottis, holding the breath outside. Maintain a comfortable and steady retention without straining. When you are ready to resume breathing, inhale slowly and deeply.
    • Tips and Caution::
  • Gradual Progression: If you are new to breath retention, start with short durations and gradually increase as you become more comfortable..
  • Listen to Your Body: Avoid forcing the breath or holding it beyond your comfort level. If you feel lightheaded or uncomfortable, release the breath immediately.
  • Health Considerations: Individuals with certain health conditions, such as cardiovascular issues or respiratory disorders, should consult with a healthcare professional before practicing Kumbhaka.
  • Guidance: If possible, learn Kumbhaka under the guidance of an experienced yoga instructor, especially if you are new to pranayama practices.
